It plays a crucial role in various tasks including internet browsing [115, 122, 201], question-answering systems [1, 111, 238], and LLM applications [53, 64, 303]. In the RAS paradigm, IR serves as a critical component that provides external, up-to-date, yet unstructured knowledge. The quality of the IR process directly impacts the overall effectiveness of the whole framework. 3.1 Classical Retrieval Methods 3.1.1 Sparse Retrieval. Sparse retrieval has long been the de facto standard for information retrieval [67, 70, 150]. At the core, it repre- sents text using normalized term frequency and inverse document frequency. Fundamental Approaches. A fundamental approach in this cate- gory is TF-IDF [229], which scores terms based on their frequency in a document while penalizing common words that appear across many documents. BM25 [210, 211], an improvement over TF-IDF, refines this approach by introducing a saturation function for term frequency and document length normalization. Statistical Modifications. Several statistical variations of BM25 have been introduced [240]. BM25F [199, 210, 226] extends BM25 for fielded search by considering term weights across multiple docu- ment fields. BM25+ [156] introduces different frequency saturation mechanisms. Other variants include BM25-adpt [155], LM-DS [284], and LM-PYP [175], each optimizing the BM25 formula for different retrieval scenarios. Neural Modifications. Learned sparse retrieval incorporates neu- ral networks to generate sparse, term-based representations [39, 58, 139, 161]. DeepCT [39] and uniCOIL [139] leverage transformers to re-weight terms, assigning weights based on semantic importance. SPLADE [58] utilizes masked language modeling to predict addi- tional relevant terms, blending semantic strengths of deep learning with efficiency of inverted indexes. Impact. Even in the deep learning era, sparse retrieval remains a strong baseline due to its effectiveness, efficiency and interpretabil- ity. It continues to be competitive with more complex neural mod- els, particularly with limited training data or domain-specific con- straints [238]. However, term-based scoring cannot retrieve relevant content without lexical overlap and can miss broader context or meaning [4, 150]. 3.1.2 Dense Retrieval. In contrast to sparse lexical methods, dense retrieval [61, 304, 314] uses continuous vector embeddings to rep- resent queries and documents. With pre-trained language mod- els [44, 180, 206], relevant query–document pairs have high cosine similarity, enabling semantic matching without lexical overlap. Dual Encoder and Early Dense Retrieval. Early dense retrieval emerged as a response to the limitations of sparse retrieval. Karpukhin et al. [111] designed Dense Passage Retrieval (DPR), which showed that a BERT [44]-based dual encoder trained on question–answer pairs [10, 11, 104, 119, 207] can surpass BM25 [210, 211] in open- domain question answering. Early models struggled with gen- eralization, requiring large-scale labeled datasets [179] and ap- propriate negative sampling strategies [266] in contrastive learn- ing [27, 51, 75, 188]. Effectively-trained Dense Retrieval. Recognizing limitations of static negative sampling, Asynchronous Negative Contrastive Esti- mation (ANCE) [266] addressed this by refreshing hard negatives during training.

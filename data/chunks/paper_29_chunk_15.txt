VII. EXPERIMENT The experimental study intends to answer the following questions: • RQ1: (1) How effective is our CARROT for the cost- constrained RAG pipeline compared to other methods? (2) Does CARROT consistently maintain its effectiveness across different LLMs? • RQ2: How efficient is CARROT compared to different baselines? • RQ3: How does CARROT perform with large-scale datasets when evaluating its scalability? • RQ4: How does the performance of CARROT vary across different cost budget constraints? • RQ5: What is the effectiveness of each component in CARROT? A. Experimental Setting Environment. We integrate our framework with the RAG framework LlamaIndex [32] and Faiss [55]. Experiments used dual Intel Xeon Gold 6326 CPUs (32 cores, 64 threads), 512 GB DDR4 RAM, and 960 GB NVMe SSD. The con- figuration agent is implemented in PyTorch 2.4, with data collection and training performed on 8×A5000 GPUs. We choose OpenRouter [56] to deploy our inference service. Datasets. To evaluate the performance of CARROT across di- verse scenarios, we conduct experiments on three datasets with differing task focuses (Table I): (1) WikiPassageQA [57] is a question-answering benchmark containing 4,165 questions and over 100,000 text chunks, designed for non-factoid question answering and passage retrieval. (2) MARCO [58] is a compre- hensive dataset tailored for natural language processing tasks, primarily emphasizing question answering and retrieval. (3) HotpotQA [59] is a question-answering benchmark designed for multi-hop reasoning tasks, consisting of approximately 113K QA pairs sourced from Wikipedia. Baselines. We compare CARROT against representative rank- based RAG methods, including RAPTOR [6], NaiveRAG [28], HYDE [29], and FLARE [17], to demonstrate improvements in retrieval relevance and response quality. We also evaluate graph-based approaches such as GraphRAG [13], Graph- CoT [25], and G-Retriever [60], highlighting our efficiency advantages. Finally, we include comparisons with tuning-based methods, despite their substantial computational overhead and infrastructure requirements. For instance, ColBERT [23] re- quires 3 hours to index 9M passages on four V100 GPUs, while RankRAG [14] and Self-RAG [21] demand 128 A100 GPUs for 16 hours and 4 A100 GPUs for 12 hours, re- spectively. We therefore include ColBERT as a representative baseline for fairness, noting that such tuning-based methods are often impractical for large-scale deployment due to their prohibitive training and indexing costs.
